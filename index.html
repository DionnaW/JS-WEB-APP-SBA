About Cats

The cat (Felis catus), commonly referred to as the domestic cat or house cat, is the only domesticated species in the family Felidae.
Recent advances in archaeology and genetics have shown that the domestication of the cat occurred in the Near East around 7500 BC.
As of 2017, the domestic cat was the second most popular pet in the United States, with 95.6 million cats owned and around 42 million households owning at least one cat.
As of 2021, there were an estimated 220 million owned and 480 million stray cats in the world.
Most cats have five claws on their front paws and four on their rear paws. The claws on the forefeet are typically sharper than those on the hindfeet.
During a fall from a high place, a cat reflexively twists its body and rights itself to land on its feet using its acute sense of balance and flexibility.
Cats have excellent night vision and can see at one sixth the light level required for human vision.
The domestic cat's hearing is most acute in the range of 500 Hz to 32 kHz. t can detect an extremely broad range of frequencies ranging from 55 Hz to 79 kHz.
Cats have an acute sense of smell; it allows them to sense certain aromas in a way that humans cannot.
Cats have relatively few taste buds compared to humans (470 or so versus more than 9,000 on the human tongue).
To aid with navigation and sensation, cats have dozens of movable whiskers (vibrissae) over their body, especially their faces.
These provide information on the width of gaps and on the location of objects in the dark, both by touching objects directly and by sensing air currents;
